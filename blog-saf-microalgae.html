The Green Skies Ahead: Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF) and Microalgae

☁️ ☀️ 🌿 ☁️ ✈️

The aviation industry currently faces a massive challenge: how to decarbonize a sector that heavily relies on energy-dense, liquid fossil fuels. While electric batteries and hydrogen show promise for ground transport, they are currently too heavy or bulky for long-haul flights. Enter Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF) — and one of its most promising, futuristic feedstocks: microalgae.

Here is a breakdown of what SAF is, why the industry needs it, and how microscopic plants might be the key to sustainable flying.

🌍 80% CO₂ emissions reduction over lifecycle
🌱 100× More oil per acre vs. soybeans
⏳ 24h Time for algae biomass to double

What is Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF)?

SAF is a liquid fuel currently used in commercial aviation that reduces CO₂ emissions by up to 80% over its lifecycle compared to traditional fossil jet fuel.

Crucially, SAF is a “drop-in” fuel. This means its chemical properties are nearly identical to conventional jet fuel. It can be blended with traditional fossil fuel and pumped directly into existing aircraft engines and airport fueling infrastructure without requiring any expensive modifications to the planes.

Currently, most commercial SAF is made from feedstocks like used cooking oil, animal fats, and municipal solid waste. However, as the demand for SAF skyrockets to meet global net-zero targets by 2050, the world will quickly run out of these waste materials. This is where microalgae step into the spotlight.

The Microalgae Connection

Microalgae are microscopic, photosynthetic organisms found in both marine and freshwater environments. They use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to grow and multiply.

So, how does pond scum relate to jet engines? The answer is lipids.

Under certain conditions, many strains of microalgae store energy in the form of natural oils (lipids). These lipids are incredibly similar in chemical structure to the hydrocarbons found in crude oil. By extracting these oils, scientists can refine them into bio-jet fuel.

How Microalgae Becomes Jet Fuel

The process of turning algae into SAF generally involves four steps:

  1. Cultivation: Algae are grown in large open ponds or closed, light-controlled tubes called photobioreactors. During this phase, they absorb massive amounts of CO₂.
  2. Harvesting: The algae are separated from the water. Because they are suspended in large volumes of liquid, this step requires significant energy (using centrifuges or filtration).
  3. Extraction: The cell walls of the algae are broken down, and the precious lipids (bio-oils) are extracted using solvents or mechanical pressing.
  4. Conversion: The extracted oil undergoes a refining process, most commonly the HEFA (Hydroprocessed Esters and Fatty Acids) pathway. Hydrogen is added to the bio-oil to remove oxygen, resulting in a pure hydrocarbon drop-in jet fuel.

Alternative: Hydrothermal Liquefaction (HTL) can also be used, which uses high heat and pressure to turn whole, wet algae directly into a “bio-crude” oil, skipping the complex extraction step.

Why Microalgae is a “Holy Grail” Feedstock

While currently in the pilot and research phases, microalgae offer several distinct advantages over other SAF feedstocks (like soybeans, palm, or corn):

  • Incredible Yields: Microalgae can grow incredibly fast, doubling their biomass in as little as 24 hours. They can produce up to 10 to 100 times more oil per acre than traditional terrestrial oil crops like soybeans.
  • No “Food vs. Fuel” Conflict: Algae do not require arable farmland or freshwater. They can be cultivated on non-productive, arid land using brackish water, seawater, or even municipal wastewater.
  • Carbon Capture: Because algae need CO₂ to grow, cultivation facilities can be hooked up directly to the exhaust flues of power plants or industrial factories, recycling carbon emissions that would otherwise enter the atmosphere.

The Reality Check: Current Challenges

While the science is proven, the economics are currently the main bottleneck. It is essential to be realistic about where the technology stands today.

The primary challenge with microalgae-derived SAF is the cost. Currently, algae-based fuel is significantly more expensive to produce than fossil jet fuel and other waste-based SAFs.

Key hurdles include:

  • High Energy Requirements: Separating tiny algae cells from large volumes of water (dewatering) is incredibly energy-intensive, which can offset the carbon benefits of the fuel if the grid isn’t powered by renewables.
  • Infrastructure Costs: Building vast commercial-scale photobioreactors or heavily monitored open-pond systems requires massive upfront capital.
  • Strain Stability: Maintaining a stable, high-lipid-producing strain of algae outdoors without it being contaminated by invasive species or predators (like microscopic zooplankton) is notoriously difficult.

The Outlook

Microalgae-derived SAF represents a massive leap forward for sustainable aviation. While it is not yet commercially viable at scale, continuous investments from both governments and the aviation sector are driving the technology forward. Innovations in genetic engineering (to make algae produce more oil) and better harvesting technologies are steadily bringing down the costs.

In the long term, once production scales and waste feedstocks max out, algae may very well be the fuel that powers the future of global flight.
